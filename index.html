Making tools for makers

Imagine that you are a violin maker. You make the most beautiful, resonant instruments -- the best sounding violins in the world.

Now imagine that all the tradesmen you know suddenly disappear. The timber merchant you buy your wood from: gone. The wire maker you buy wire from to make your strings. The man down the road who sells varnish. All of them are no more. What's more, you can't find anyone else who makes these things. The entire supply chain for your violin making business is gone. But you think making violins is really important, so you set about learning their trades.

It would take several lifetimes to acquire all the necessary skills. And even if you somehow managed it, how much time would you have left to spend on what really mattered? The sound of your violins.

As a violinmaker, you exist to serve violinists, and through them, the audience.

We'll come back to violins later. What about a field you probably know more about? How many of you have ever written JavaScript?

If you write JavaScript, you probably know something about the DOM programming model, built into every browser. Each browser runs on an Operating System which has its heart a kernel, which is almost certainly written in a programming language called C, and compiled with a C compiler: a large and very clever program such as GCC, or the IBM C compiler. That C compiler has a detailed understanding of the machine code understood by the particular processor in your computer. The machine code itself triggers the action of thousands of electronic components inside the processor. Each of these components may include hundreds of thousands of transistors. To understand a transistor you need a decent understanding of semiconductor physics, which in turn requires a not inconsiderable knowledge of...

So how do we ever get anything done?

The Black Box

The answer is Black Box Abstraction: the process of wrapping complex things up into a box that hides most of the details of their construction, and idealises their behaviour so that you can reason about them.

Our violin maker doesn't know how to fell a tree, and he doesn't know how to make an axe, but he has commercial relationships with people who do. And none of the complexity of forestry or axemaking leaks into those commercial relationships. So he doesn't have to worry.

And you, coding JavaScript, don't need to understand QM because Black Box Abstraction is *everywhere* in the technological world. The DOM is a BBA. Functions are a BBA. Object-oriented programming is a BBA. Assembly language is a BBA. Infrastructure as a Service is a BBA. Every single API ever written is a BBA.

Except SOAP, which is the only known abstraction that's more complicated than the system it represents.

Black Box Abstractions allow you to reason about systems of unreasonable complexity, and they are *the* central paradigm for technological advance. As soon as a technology or process can be packaged up in a black box through which it exposes a small but useful fraction of its inner complexity, its users can concentrate on *using* it, rather than trying to understand how it was made.

How do we make a successful box?

Well, the first thing you need to do is Stop. Stop and think. Think then think harder and then think some more. There is no "quick win" for this, when I rule the world that phrase "quick win" will be illegal. Think about what black boxes can you draw around functions at work? Don't think job titles, think areas of problems/know-how. The lines will be fuzzy in places and that's OK. It takes time to figure out where the edges of the box are and it may contain other boxes within it. I'm not talking on a macro level of what coding language to use, which tools and processes to put in place, I'm talking about the secret ingredient... People.

GOV.UK ALPHA

When the idea of the alpha of GOV.UK was being baked, those discussing its possiblity took the time to think about what flavour of people they'd need. People like Tom Looosemore don't particularly have in-depth technology know-how, what they had was an understanding of the importance of each of the black boxes needed and to what depth of know-how was needed given the incredibly short time scale. They thought about it some more, found the edges and then they pulled together a team of about a dozen people who in 12 weeks successfuly built the alpha. There was much rejoicing. Then shit got real. How do you scale from a team of 12 to what ended up being a team of around 140

Rock stars need not apply

Recognising how important it is to assemble a good team and doing so is a skill in its own right. Time was taken to re-evaluate what flavours of people were needed to build on that success and make GOV.UK "for real". There seems to me, a common misconception in tech that you need rock stars to deliver, faster better stronger code. This is a lie. No one person can carry the success of your product/team and to think like that is introducing a single point of failure. Get a team of rockstars together and you'll have a disaster. A faster, worse-erer disastrous fuck up. I'm tired of seeing the same description of what makes for "great developers", like carbon clones of each other who live and breathe code and drink from the same ego-boosting kool aid. This is bullshit. There is not a single rock star in sight at GDS, this was no accident.

Good team needs diverse good people

Building things is hard. It’s a balance between ideal and pragmatic. You’ve got to find the edges of the blackbox and adapt accordingly or hire people that will and then trust them to go do that. There is no silver bullet and what works for one problem doesn't work for another. Scripts that were hacked together to build a vm during the beta were no longer susatinable. We had to continually iterate and scale the tools and process to fit the ever expanding new team. We didn't always have the "expertise", that didn't stop us because we had the right flavour of people who are intelligent, fun and above all respectful. We worked together and figured stuff out as best we could. On paper, going from the beta to LIVE, shouldn't really have ever have worked. It was insane. It was hard work. It was only possible because we never lost sight of the most important thing...
