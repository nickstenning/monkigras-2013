Now imagine that all the tradesmen you know suddenly disappear. The timber merchant you buy your wood from: gone. The wire maker you buy wire from to make your strings. But you think making violins is really important, so you set about learning their trades.

What about a field you probably know more about? How many of you have ever written JavaScript?

If you write JavaScript, you probably know something about the DOM programming model, built into every browser. Each browser runs on an Operating System which has its heart a kernel, which is almost certainly written in a programming language called C, and compiled with a C compiler: a large and very clever program such as GCC, or the IBM C compiler. That C compiler has a detailed understanding of the machine code understood by the particular processor in your computer. The machine code itself triggers the action of thousands of electronic components inside the processor. Each of these components may include hundreds of thousands of transistors. To understand a transistor you need a decent understanding of semiconductor physics, which in turn requires a not inconsiderable knowledge of...

The Black Box

The answer is Black Box Abstraction: the process of wrapping complex things up into a box that hides most of the details of their construction, and idealises their behaviour so that you can reason about them.

Black Box Abstraction is *everywhere* in the technological world. Assembly language is a BBA. Functions in languages like C are a BBA. Object-oriented programming is a BBA. Infrastructure as a Service is a BBA. Every single API ever written is a BBA.

Except SOAP, which is the only known abstraction that's more complicated than the system it represents.

Black Box Abstractions allow you to reason about systems of unreasonable complexity. The BBA is *the* central paradigm for technological advance. As soon as a technology or process can be packaged up in a black box through which it exposes a small but useful fraction of its inner complexity, its users can concentrate on *using* it, rather than trying to understand how it was made.

Understanding variety of depth of expertise

Building things is hard. It’s important to take the time to really understand what the right level of expertise your team or company needs, whether that’s building software, brewing beer or printing a steam train. You don’t have to understand all of the details, understand enough which bits are critical that need some level of pre-existing experience and which bits there’s room to grow or consult others for advice. It’s a balance between ideal and pragmatic. You’ve got to find the edges of the blackbox and adapt accordingly or hire people that will and then trust them to go do that.

Rockstars need not apply

Let’s say you’ve got to build something quickly, I can see the allure of the silver bullet that if only you had 10 rockstar developers you’d get it done faster, stronger, better. Sorry folks, that’s just not true. I’m not saying that 10 novices are better than 1 expert, I’m saying that you need a diverse mix. If the team doesn’t play well together, you’re fucked. If you don’t put trust in the people you’ve chosen, you’re fucked. If you have a team full of identical people with little diversity, you’re fucked. Put your faith in rockstars and you’re fucked.

User needs a Great Team

Many companies talk about that it’s “all about the team”, few actually live by that. Whether you’re making, selling, maintaining, developing or pulling off a heist. They all need good people. Only by the power of greyskull, no wait, power of collective intelligence of good people make a great team.
